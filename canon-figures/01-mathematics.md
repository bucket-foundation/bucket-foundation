# 01 — Mathematics

> **What counts as canon-tier in mathematics.** A foundation in mathematics is one of: a new axiomatic system, a primary derivation that opens a discipline, a method (algebra, calculus, set theory, category theory) that becomes the substrate of subsequent work, or a proof that resolves a foundational question (consistency, completeness, undecidability). Refinement of an existing method, no matter how technically deep, is not canon-tier. The figures below each added a foundation, not a refinement.

---

### Euclid of Alexandria

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | euclid |
| **lifespan** | c.325–c.265 BCE |
| **era** | Hellenistic |
| **region / tradition** | Alexandria, Ptolemaic Egypt — Greek geometry |
| **branches** | 01-mathematics |
| **primary works** | *Elements* (c.300 BCE, Koine Greek) |

**Foundation contribution.** Codified the **axiomatic-deductive method**: start from a small set of explicit postulates, derive everything else by proof. The *Elements* is not the first geometry; it is the first geometry that says *here are my assumptions, here are my rules of inference, watch me build the rest.*

**Why canon.** Defines what "a mathematical foundation" *is* for the next 2,300 years. Every later axiomatic system (Hilbert, Tarski, Bourbaki, ZFC) is downstream of this template. Without Euclid there is no notion that mathematics needs foundations at all.

**Downstream.** Hilbert (Foundations of Geometry, 1899), Bourbaki, modern proof theory, every textbook called *Foundations of X*.

**Tags.** `axiomatic-method` `geometry` `proof` `foundation-of-foundations`

### Archimedes of Syracuse

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | archimedes |
| **lifespan** | c.287–c.212 BCE |
| **era** | Hellenistic |
| **region / tradition** | Syracuse, Magna Graecia — Greek geometry & mechanics |
| **branches** | 01-mathematics |
| **cross-branches** | 02-physics |
| **primary works** | *On the Sphere and Cylinder*, *On Floating Bodies*, *The Method* (c.250 BCE, Koine Greek) |

**Foundation contribution.** Developed **the method of exhaustion** — a rigorous limiting procedure for areas and volumes that is the historical ancestor of integral calculus. *The Method* (rediscovered in the Archimedes Palimpsest, 1906) shows him using infinitesimals heuristically and then proving the result by exhaustion, anticipating both calculus and the standard "guess-then-rigorize" method of modern math.

**Why canon.** Calculus did not have to wait until Newton and Leibniz; the conceptual move was already here. Archimedes is the proof that limiting arguments and quasi-infinitesimal reasoning are foundational, not merely useful.

**Downstream.** Cavalieri (indivisibles, 1635), Newton, Leibniz, the entire history of analysis.

**Disputed.** *The Method* was lost for 1,700 years and only recovered in 1906 from a palimpsest under a Byzantine prayer book. Most of mathematics rebuilt his result without knowing he had found it first.

**Tags.** `exhaustion` `limit` `quadrature` `integral-calculus-prefiguration`

### Brahmagupta

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | brahmagupta |
| **lifespan** | 598–668 CE |
| **era** | Classical India |
| **region / tradition** | Bhillamala, Gurjara kingdom — Indian mathematics & astronomy |
| **branches** | 01-mathematics |
| **cross-branches** | 06-cosmology |
| **primary works** | *Brāhmasphuṭasiddhānta* (628 CE, Sanskrit) |

**Foundation contribution.** Gave the **first systematic rules for arithmetic with zero and negative numbers**, including the (then-novel) statement that a debt minus a debt is a debt and that zero divided by zero is undefined. Stated the general solution of the quadratic and gave the cyclic-quadrilateral area formula that bears his name.

**Why canon.** Zero as a *number* (not just a placeholder) is the foundational move that makes algebra tractable. Without it, al-Khwarizmi's algebra has nothing to operate on. The European reception came via Arabic translation 200+ years later.

**Downstream.** al-Khwarizmi (via translation chains), Bhāskara II, Fibonacci's *Liber Abaci* (1202), modern arithmetic.

**Tags.** `zero` `negative-numbers` `quadratic` `indian-mathematics`

### Muḥammad ibn Mūsā al-Khwārizmī

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | al-khwarizmi |
| **lifespan** | c.780–c.850 CE |
| **era** | Islamic Golden Age |
| **region / tradition** | Baghdad, House of Wisdom — Arabic mathematics |
| **branches** | 01-mathematics |
| **primary works** | *al-Kitāb al-mukhtaṣar fī ḥisāb al-jabr wal-muqābala* (c.820 CE, Arabic); *On the Calculation with Hindu Numerals* (c.825 CE) |

**Foundation contribution.** Founded **algebra** as a discipline distinct from arithmetic and geometry. The word *algebra* is *al-jabr* ("restoration"). The word *algorithm* is a Latinization of his name. He systematized the procedure of representing unknowns symbolically and operating on them by rules, and he introduced the Hindu (decimal-positional) numerals to the Arabic-reading world.

**Why canon.** Algebra is the substrate of every later mathematical discipline. The notion of *operating on unknowns by rules* is a foundation, not a technique. The Latin Europe of 1200–1500 inherited the entire computational toolkit through this single channel.

**Downstream.** Fibonacci, Cardano, Viète, Descartes, every symbolic system in mathematics and computer science.

**Tags.** `algebra` `algorithm` `decimal-numerals` `islamic-golden-age`

### Carl Friedrich Gauss

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | gauss |
| **lifespan** | 1777–1855 |
| **era** | Late Enlightenment / 19th-century Europe |
| **region / tradition** | Göttingen — German mathematics |
| **branches** | 01-mathematics |
| **cross-branches** | 02-physics, 06-cosmology |
| **primary works** | *Disquisitiones Arithmeticae* (1801, Latin); *Theoria Motus Corporum Coelestium* (1809, Latin); *Disquisitiones Generales circa Superficies Curvas* (1827, Latin) |

**Foundation contribution.** Three foundations in three different branches: (1) **modern number theory** in the *Disquisitiones* — congruences, quadratic reciprocity, the structure of  $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$; (2) **intrinsic differential geometry** with the *Theorema Egregium*, the result that curvature can be defined without reference to an embedding, which Riemann then generalized into the substrate of general relativity; (3) the **method of least squares** as a principled foundation for statistical estimation.

**Why canon.** Each of the three is sufficient by itself for canon status. Together they make Gauss one of the rare figures who built foundations in three branches simultaneously.

**Downstream.** Riemann (Riemannian geometry, 1854), Dedekind, Hilbert, Einstein (via Riemann), all of modern statistics.

**Tags.** `number-theory` `differential-geometry` `least-squares` `theorema-egregium`

### Évariste Galois

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | galois |
| **lifespan** | 1811–1832 |
| **era** | Restoration France |
| **region / tradition** | Paris — French mathematics |
| **branches** | 01-mathematics |
| **primary works** | *Mémoire sur les conditions de résolubilité des équations par radicaux* (1831, French; published posthumously 1846) |

**Foundation contribution.** Founded **group theory** by recognizing that the question *which polynomials are solvable by radicals* is really a question about the *symmetry group* of the polynomial's roots. The shift from "solve this equation" to "what is the structure of the symmetries of its solution set" is the foundational move.

**Why canon.** Group theory is the language of symmetry across all of mathematics and physics. Noether's theorem (energy conservation ↔ time-translation symmetry), Lie groups, the Standard Model, crystallography, knot theory, Galois cohomology — all downstream.

**Disputed.** Wrote the foundational paper the night before the duel that killed him at age 20. The manuscript was illegible enough that it took Liouville fourteen years to publish it (1846). The discipline of group theory therefore began with a fourteen-year delay.

**Tags.** `group-theory` `symmetry` `solvability` `posthumous-canon`

### Bernhard Riemann

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | riemann |
| **lifespan** | 1826–1866 |
| **era** | 19th-century Europe |
| **region / tradition** | Göttingen — German mathematics |
| **branches** | 01-mathematics |
| **cross-branches** | 02-physics, 06-cosmology |
| **primary works** | *Über die Hypothesen, welche der Geometrie zu Grunde liegen* (1854, German); *Über die Anzahl der Primzahlen unter einer gegebenen Größe* (1859, German) |

**Foundation contribution.** Two foundations in twelve years: (1) **Riemannian geometry** — the inaugural lecture of 1854 generalizes Gauss's intrinsic geometry to manifolds of arbitrary dimension and arbitrary curvature, providing the mathematical substrate Einstein needed for general relativity 60 years later; (2) the **Riemann zeta function and the Riemann hypothesis** — the 1859 paper connects the distribution of primes to the analytic behavior of $\zeta(s)$, defining the entire field of analytic number theory and stating the still-open conjecture that organizes it.

**Why canon.** Riemannian geometry made GR possible. The zeta function reframes prime numbers as a problem in complex analysis. Both were single-step foundational moves.

**Downstream.** Einstein, Poincaré, Hilbert, every analytic number theorist of the 20th century.

**Tags.** `riemannian-geometry` `zeta-function` `manifold` `analytic-number-theory`

### Georg Cantor

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | cantor |
| **lifespan** | 1845–1918 |
| **era** | Late 19th / early 20th century |
| **region / tradition** | Halle — German set theory |
| **branches** | 01-mathematics |
| **primary works** | *Über eine Eigenschaft des Inbegriffes aller reellen algebraischen Zahlen* (1874, German); *Beiträge zur Begründung der transfiniten Mengenlehre* (1895/97, German) |

**Foundation contribution.** Founded **set theory** and proved that **infinity comes in different sizes** — the diagonal argument shows that the real numbers are uncountable while the rationals are countable, and the iterated power-set construction yields a strictly increasing tower of infinite cardinals $\aleph_0, \aleph_1, \aleph_2, \ldots$.

**Why canon.** Set theory becomes the substrate for the entire 20th-century rebuild of mathematics (ZFC). The diagonal argument becomes the template for every later impossibility proof, including Gödel's incompleteness theorems and Turing's halting problem.

**Downstream.** Zermelo, Fraenkel, Hilbert, Gödel, Turing, every undecidability proof in logic and CS.

**Tags.** `set-theory` `cardinality` `diagonal-argument` `transfinite`

### David Hilbert

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | hilbert |
| **lifespan** | 1862–1943 |
| **era** | Early 20th century |
| **region / tradition** | Göttingen — German mathematics |
| **branches** | 01-mathematics |
| **primary works** | *Grundlagen der Geometrie* (1899, German); *Mathematische Probleme* (1900, German — the 23 problems) |

**Foundation contribution.** Re-founded geometry on a fully formal axiomatic basis, replacing Euclid's intuitive postulates with a system that no longer relied on visual intuition. Then, in the 1900 ICM lecture, posed **23 problems** that organized the research agenda of 20th-century mathematics, several of which (the second — consistency of arithmetic, the eighth — Riemann hypothesis, the tenth — diophantine decidability) are foundational rather than technical.

**Why canon.** Hilbert is the figure who explicitly made *foundations* a research program. The Hilbert program (prove arithmetic consistent within arithmetic) is the program Gödel was responding to.

**Downstream.** Gödel (incompleteness, 1931), Turing, the entire foundational research community of the 20th century.

**Tags.** `formalism` `axiomatic-geometry` `hilbert-problems` `foundations-program`

### Kurt Gödel

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | godel |
| **lifespan** | 1906–1978 |
| **era** | 20th century |
| **region / tradition** | Vienna → Princeton (IAS) — mathematical logic |
| **branches** | 01-mathematics |
| **cross-branches** | 04-information, 07-mind |
| **primary works** | *Über formal unentscheidbare Sätze der Principia Mathematica und verwandter Systeme I* (1931, German) |

**Foundation contribution.** Proved the **two incompleteness theorems**: (1) any consistent formal system rich enough to express arithmetic contains true statements it cannot prove; (2) such a system cannot prove its own consistency from within. The proof technique — encoding statements about the system *as numbers in the system* (Gödel numbering) — is itself a foundational move that becomes the template for self-reference in computer science (Turing's halting problem, Kleene's recursion theorem).

**Why canon.** Closes the Hilbert program by demonstrating that the program is impossible. The result is not a refinement; it is a structural limit on what formal systems can do.

**Downstream.** Turing, Church, Kleene, Tarski, all of computability theory and mathematical logic.

**Tags.** `incompleteness` `self-reference` `godel-numbering` `formal-systems`
