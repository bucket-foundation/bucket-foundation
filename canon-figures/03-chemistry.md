# 03 — Chemistry

> **What counts as canon-tier in chemistry.** A foundational identification (element, compound, bond type, reaction mechanism), a method that opens an entire class of synthesis or analysis, a periodic structure that organizes the field, or a quantitative law (mass action, atomic weights, conservation of mass). Refining a yield is not canon-tier. The figures below each added a structural piece.

---

### Antoine Lavoisier

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | lavoisier |
| **lifespan** | 1743–1794 |
| **era** | Late Enlightenment / French Revolution |
| **region / tradition** | Paris — French chemistry |
| **primary works** | *Traité Élémentaire de Chimie* (1789, French); *Réflexions sur le phlogistique* (1783, French) |

**Foundation contribution.** Established the **conservation of mass in chemical reactions** by careful weighing experiments on combustion. Demolished the phlogiston theory by showing that combustion is *combination with oxygen*, not loss of phlogiston. Co-introduced systematic chemical nomenclature (oxygen, hydrogen, etc.) — naming a substance after what it does, not after where it came from. The *Traité* is the founding textbook of modern chemistry.

**Why canon.** Chemistry becomes a quantitative science here. Before Lavoisier, the discipline is alchemy with better instruments; after, it has a conservation law and a rational naming system.

**Downstream.** Dalton, Berzelius, Mendeleev, every quantitative chemist.

**Disputed.** Executed by guillotine in 1794 (*"the Republic has no need of savants"*) at age 50.

**Tags.** `oxygen` `combustion` `mass-conservation` `nomenclature`

### John Dalton

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | dalton |
| **lifespan** | 1766–1844 |
| **era** | Early 19th century |
| **region / tradition** | Manchester — English chemistry |
| **primary works** | *A New System of Chemical Philosophy* (1808, English) |

**Foundation contribution.** Proposed the **modern atomic theory of chemistry**: each element consists of identical atoms with a characteristic atomic weight, compounds form by atoms combining in small whole-number ratios, and chemical reactions are rearrangements of atoms (mass conservation follows trivially). Provided the first table of relative atomic weights.

**Why canon.** Atoms had been postulated since Democritus. Dalton made them *quantitative* and *predictive* — the law of multiple proportions falls out, and chemistry can finally calculate.

**Downstream.** Avogadro, Cannizzaro, Mendeleev.

**Tags.** `atomic-theory` `multiple-proportions` `atomic-weights` `quantitative-chemistry`

### Dmitri Mendeleev

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | mendeleev |
| **lifespan** | 1834–1907 |
| **era** | Late 19th century |
| **region / tradition** | Saint Petersburg — Russian chemistry |
| **primary works** | *Опыт системы элементов, основанной на их атомном весе и химическом сходстве* (1869, Russian); *Основы химии* (1868–71, Russian) |

**Foundation contribution.** Constructed the **periodic table** by ordering elements by atomic weight and noticing that chemical properties recur at regular intervals. Crucially, **left gaps** for undiscovered elements and *predicted their properties* (eka-aluminum = gallium, eka-silicon = germanium). The predictions were confirmed within fifteen years.

**Why canon.** The periodic table is one of the most successful organizing structures in any science. The fact that the gaps could be filled with predicted elements is the strongest possible empirical confirmation that the structure is foundational, not arbitrary.

**Downstream.** Moseley (atomic-number ordering, 1913), Bohr (electron shells, 1913), the entire structural chemistry of the 20th century.

**Tags.** `periodic-table` `periodicity` `prediction` `eka-elements`

### Marie Skłodowska-Curie

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | curie |
| **lifespan** | 1867–1934 |
| **era** | Turn of the 20th century |
| **region / tradition** | Paris — Polish/French radiochemistry |
| **branches** | 03-chemistry |
| **cross-branches** | 02-physics |
| **primary works** | *Recherches sur les substances radioactives* (1903, French — doctoral thesis) |

**Foundation contribution.** Co-founded **radiochemistry**. Coined the word *radioactivity*. Isolated polonium and radium from tonnes of pitchblende and showed that radioactivity is an *atomic* property, not a chemical one — an atom of radium emits radiation regardless of what compound it is in. This was the first evidence that atoms have internal structure that can change.

**Why canon.** The first crack in the indivisibility of the atom. Without this, the Rutherford nuclear model and the entire 20th-century nuclear/atomic enterprise has no entry point.

**Downstream.** Rutherford, Soddy (isotopes), the entire field of nuclear chemistry.

**Tags.** `radioactivity` `polonium` `radium` `atomic-property`

### Linus Pauling

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | pauling |
| **lifespan** | 1901–1994 |
| **era** | 20th century |
| **region / tradition** | Caltech — American chemistry |
| **branches** | 03-chemistry |
| **cross-branches** | 05-biophysics |
| **primary works** | *The Nature of the Chemical Bond* (1939, English); papers on protein structure (1951) |

**Foundation contribution.** Founded **quantum chemistry** by applying quantum mechanics to chemical bonding — hybridization, resonance, electronegativity, and the explanation of why bonds have the lengths and angles they do. *The Nature of the Chemical Bond* (1939) is the founding text of structural chemistry. Later, with Robert Corey, predicted the **alpha helix and beta sheet** as the secondary structures of proteins (1951), foundational to molecular biology.

**Why canon.** Bonding goes from empirical patterns to quantum-mechanical derivation. Two foundations in two branches.

**Downstream.** All of structural chemistry; Watson and Crick (who studied Pauling's helical-structure paper while modeling DNA).

**Tags.** `chemical-bond` `electronegativity` `alpha-helix` `quantum-chemistry`

### Frederick Sanger

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | sanger |
| **lifespan** | 1918–2013 |
| **era** | 20th century |
| **region / tradition** | Cambridge — English biochemistry |
| **branches** | 03-chemistry |
| **cross-branches** | 05-biophysics |
| **primary works** | Insulin sequencing papers (1955, English); *DNA sequencing with chain-terminating inhibitors* (1977, English) |

**Foundation contribution.** Two foundational *methods*, one for proteins and one for DNA. (1) **Sequenced insulin** (1955), proving for the first time that proteins have *defined sequences* — they are not statistical mixtures. This made the central dogma sequence-based and made all later structural biology possible. (2) **Sanger sequencing** (1977), the chain-termination method that became the workhorse of DNA sequencing for thirty years and underwrote the Human Genome Project.

**Why canon.** Each method opened a discipline. The first proved that biological macromolecules carry information in their *sequence*, which is the central claim of molecular biology. The second made that information *readable* at scale.

**Downstream.** All of molecular biology; the Human Genome Project; modern genomics.

**Tags.** `insulin-sequence` `protein-sequencing` `dna-sequencing` `chain-termination`
