# 09 — Art

> **What counts as canon-tier in art.** A foundational form (epic, sonnet, fugue, ukiyo-e print, novel-of-consciousness), a technical innovation that becomes the substrate of subsequent work (perspective, equal temperament, modernist montage), or an individual work whose influence so reorganizes its medium that the medium afterward is recognizably *post-X*. Refining a style is not canon-tier; the figures below each forced or enabled a structural reframing of their medium.
>
> **Editorial note.** This branch is added in pass 1 to honor the *build history* slogan; the strict canon defined in `PROTOCOL.md` is foundations-only and does not include the arts. Inclusion here is for the contributor index, not for the strict canon.

### Homer — *attributed*

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | homer |
| **lifespan** | composed c.8th century BCE; possibly composite |
| **era** | Archaic Greece |
| **region / tradition** | Ionian Greek oral epic tradition |
| **primary works** | *Iliad*, *Odyssey* (c.750–700 BCE, Homeric Greek) |

**Foundation contribution.** Founding texts of the **Western literary tradition**: long-form narrative epic with elaborated character, divine machinery, formal hexameter, and ring-composition structure. The *Iliad* establishes the model of tragic action set against an unyielding fate; the *Odyssey* establishes the model of the journey of return. Almost every later epic tradition in the Mediterranean and European world is in conversation with these two poems.

**Why canon.** The texts have been continuously read, taught, translated, and adapted for ~2,800 years. Whatever they are aesthetically, they are *foundationally* one of the longest continuous transmissions in human cultural history.

**Disputed.** Authorship is unresolved. The *Homeric Question* — whether a single poet composed both poems or whether they are products of an oral tradition stitched together — has been debated since antiquity. Parry and Lord's 20th-century fieldwork on living oral epic traditions (Yugoslavia) shifted the consensus toward composite-oral origin.

**Tags.** `epic` `iliad` `odyssey` `oral-tradition`

### Sappho

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | sappho |
| **lifespan** | c.630–c.570 BCE |
| **era** | Archaic Greece |
| **region / tradition** | Lesbos — Aeolic Greek lyric poetry |
| **primary works** | Lyric fragments (collected as the *Sapphic corpus*, mostly lost; ~650 surviving lines and fragments, Aeolic Greek) |

**Foundation contribution.** Founded **lyric poetry as the first-person representation of subjective experience** in the Western tradition. Her surviving fragments are short, intensely personal, formally rigorous, and emotionally unfiltered. The *Sapphic stanza* (a four-line metrical form) bears her name and was imitated by Catullus, Horace, and many later poets. She is one of the earliest figures in the Western canon to write the *interior life* in the first person.

**Why canon.** The lyric subject — the *I* who feels and speaks — is one of the inventions of literature. Sappho is one of its founders.

**Disputed.** Most of her work was lost in antiquity, with only a single complete poem and ~650 lines surviving in fragments, often quoted by other authors or recovered from papyri. New fragments were recovered in the 20th and early 21st centuries.

**Tags.** `lyric` `sapphic-stanza` `first-person` `lost-corpus`

### Du Fu (杜甫)

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | du-fu |
| **lifespan** | 712–770 CE |
| **era** | Tang dynasty |
| **region / tradition** | Tang-era China — Chinese regulated verse |
| **primary works** | The collected poems (~1,500 surviving, Classical Chinese) |

**Foundation contribution.** The most technically demanding and historically referential figure in the **Chinese poetic canon**. Du Fu's mature work — written during and after the An Lushan rebellion that nearly destroyed the Tang — established the standard of Chinese literary seriousness: precise observation, historical consciousness, compressed emotional range, and rigorous adherence to the formal constraints of *jintishi* (regulated verse). He is conventionally referred to in Chinese criticism as 詩聖, the "poet-sage."

**Why canon.** The reference standard against which the entire Chinese literary tradition has measured itself for 1,200 years. The classical Chinese literary education was unimaginable without him.

**Downstream.** Almost every later Chinese poet; Japanese kanshi tradition; modern Chinese literature; Eliot, Pound, Rexroth (in translation reception).

**Tags.** `tang-poetry` `regulated-verse` `poet-sage` `an-lushan`

### Dante Alighieri

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | dante |
| **lifespan** | 1265–1321 |
| **era** | Late Medieval / early Renaissance |
| **region / tradition** | Florence, in exile — Italian vernacular literature |
| **primary works** | *Commedia* (later: *Divina Commedia*, c.1308–1320, Italian); *De Vulgari Eloquentia* (c.1302–1305, Latin); *Vita Nuova* (c.1294, Italian) |

**Foundation contribution.** Wrote the *Commedia* in **Tuscan Italian rather than Latin**, against the entire learned-language tradition of the medieval West, and produced a poem so formally and intellectually ambitious that it effectively *founded* modern Italian as a literary language. The poem synthesizes scholastic theology (it is structurally a Thomistic universe), Aristotelian cosmology, classical literature, and personal political vendetta into a single architectural structure.

**Why canon.** The single most influential vernacular text in the European tradition. Italian as a literary language is *post-Dante* in the same sense that physics is *post-Newton*.

**Downstream.** All Italian literature; the entire vernacular-literature movement of the European Renaissance; T. S. Eliot's *Waste Land* (which is in many places a Dantean rewrite); modern world literature.

**Tags.** `divina-commedia` `tuscan-italian` `vernacular` `terza-rima`

### William Shakespeare

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | shakespeare |
| **lifespan** | 1564–1616 |
| **era** | Elizabethan / Jacobean England |
| **region / tradition** | London — English Renaissance theater |
| **primary works** | The 38 plays and 154 sonnets (c.1590–1613, Early Modern English) |

**Foundation contribution.** Combined **dramatic action with interiorized characterization** at a scale and depth no previous European playwright had reached. The soliloquies of *Hamlet*, *Lear*, *Macbeth*, and *Othello* are among the earliest sustained representations in Western literature of a character thinking *to themselves*, in real time, about their own situation. The result is not a formal innovation in meter but a **structural innovation in what a fictional human can be on a stage**.

**Why canon.** The reference standard of the English literary tradition; one of the most translated, performed, and reinterpreted bodies of work in any language.

**Disputed.** The "authorship question" (Marlowe, Bacon, de Vere, etc.) is not taken seriously by the historical scholarly mainstream. The corpus as transmitted is the canonical object.

**Downstream.** Almost every subsequent English-language playwright and novelist; world theater; the entire convention of the modern interiorized fictional character.

**Tags.** `tragedy` `hamlet` `interiority` `early-modern-english`

### Johann Sebastian Bach

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | bach |
| **lifespan** | 1685–1750 |
| **era** | High Baroque |
| **region / tradition** | Eisenach, Weimar, Köthen, Leipzig — German Lutheran sacred music |
| **branches** | 09-art |
| **primary works** | *Das wohltemperierte Klavier* I and II (1722, 1742); *Die Kunst der Fuge* (c.1740–1750); *Matthäus-Passion* (1727); the cantata cycle (~200 surviving) |

**Foundation contribution.** The high-water mark of **counterpoint** as a compositional method. *Das wohltemperierte Klavier* — 24 preludes and fugues in each major and minor key — established the practical case for **equal temperament** as a tuning system, opening the harmonic space that Western music explored for the next 200 years. *Die Kunst der Fuge* is the most rigorous single exploration of fugal technique ever written.

**Why canon.** Reference standard for compositional craftsmanship in the Western art-music tradition. After Bach, every serious Western composer has had to reckon with the technical bar he set.

**Downstream.** Mozart (who studied Bach late in life); Beethoven; Mendelssohn (who revived the *Matthäus-Passion* in 1829); Brahms; Schoenberg; almost every later composer of contrapuntal music.

**Tags.** `counterpoint` `fugue` `equal-temperament` `well-tempered-clavier`

### Katsushika Hokusai

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | hokusai |
| **lifespan** | 1760–1849 |
| **era** | Late Edo Japan |
| **region / tradition** | Edo — Japanese ukiyo-e woodblock printmaking |
| **primary works** | *Thirty-six Views of Mount Fuji* (c.1830–1832, Japanese); *Hokusai Manga* (1814–1878 across 15 volumes) |

**Foundation contribution.** Re-founded **ukiyo-e** as a serious aesthetic medium with the *Thirty-six Views* — landscape printmaking that combined the rigorous compositional discipline of classical Japanese painting with the popular accessibility of the woodblock print. The single image *The Great Wave off Kanagawa* became one of the most reproduced and widely recognized images in human history. *Hokusai Manga* is a 15-volume sketchbook of every imaginable subject and is foundational to the visual vocabulary of later Japanese cartooning.

**Why canon.** When Japanese prints reached Paris in the 1860s–1880s (*Japonisme*), they decisively reshaped European art — Manet, Degas, Whistler, Van Gogh, Monet, Klimt, and many others were directly indebted to Hokusai and his contemporaries. He is one of the few non-Western figures to have fundamentally restructured the visual vocabulary of Western modern art.

**Downstream.** All of late ukiyo-e; *Japonisme*; French Impressionism and Post-Impressionism; modern Japanese visual culture from prints to manga.

**Tags.** `ukiyo-e` `great-wave` `japonisme` `woodblock-print`

### Jorge Luis Borges

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | borges |
| **lifespan** | 1899–1986 |
| **era** | 20th century |
| **region / tradition** | Buenos Aires — Argentine fantastic literature |
| **branches** | 09-art |
| **cross-branches** | 04-information, 07-mind |
| **primary works** | *Ficciones* (1944, Spanish); *El Aleph* (1949, Spanish); *Otras inquisiciones* (1952, Spanish) |

**Foundation contribution.** Founded a distinctly **20th-century mode of short fiction** in which the story is structurally a philosophical or mathematical conceit — infinite libraries, branching gardens, mirrors and labyrinths, books that contain all possible books. The stories are short, formally precise, intellectually dense, and willing to treat the reader as an equal in the philosophical game.

**Why canon.** Borges is one of the small number of 20th-century writers whose influence is felt across literature *and* across philosophy *and* across computer science. *The Library of Babel* and *The Garden of Forking Paths* prefigure ideas about combinatorics, branching computation, and the multiverse that mathematicians and physicists have since formalized.

**Downstream.** All of Latin American magical realism; postmodern fiction (Calvino, Eco, Pavić, Bolaño); hypertext theory; popular philosophy of identity, time, and infinity.

**Tags.** `ficciones` `library-of-babel` `garden-of-forking-paths` `metafiction`

### Leonardo da Vinci

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | leonardo |
| **lifespan** | 1452–1519 |
| **era** | Italian Renaissance |
| **region / tradition** | Florence, Milan, Amboise — Florentine workshop practice |
| **branches** | 09-art |
| **cross-branches** | 02-physics, 05-biophysics, 10-earth |
| **primary works** | Notebooks (c.1478–1519, Italian, mirror script); *Last Supper* (1495–1498); *Mona Lisa* (c.1503–1519); *Treatise on Painting* (posthumous compilation, Italian) |

**Foundation contribution.** As painter: codified *sfumato* and atmospheric perspective as systematic techniques, and structured the picture plane around geometric-optical construction rather than iconographic convention. As notebook-keeper: produced the earliest sustained body of work treating **painting, anatomy, hydraulics, flight, and geology as a single unified investigation of visible form**. The anatomical drawings (based on personal dissection) and the water/fluid-dynamics studies are foundation-tier on their own terms.

**Why canon.** The integration itself is the foundation. Leonardo's notebooks are the most sustained early-modern example of the thesis that bucket-foundation names in 2026 — one mind operating across multiple canon branches simultaneously, in a method where the observational eye is the shared instrument.

**Downstream.** Italian High Renaissance painting; Vesalius (directly, via shared anatomical practice); the entire notebook-as-research-method tradition.

**Disputed.** The notebooks circulated posthumously in fragmentary form; the *Treatise on Painting* is a compilation by Melzi. Several attributions (*Salvator Mundi*) remain open.

**Tags.** `sfumato` `anatomy` `hydraulics` `notebook-method` `polymath`

### J. R. R. Tolkien

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | tolkien |
| **lifespan** | 1892–1973 |
| **era** | 20th century |
| **region / tradition** | Oxford — English philology and literature |
| **branches** | 09-art |
| **cross-branches** | 08-tradition |
| **primary works** | *The Lord of the Rings* (1954–55, English); *The Silmarillion* (posthumous, 1977, English); *Beowulf: The Monsters and the Critics* (1936, English) |

**Foundation contribution.** Founded **secondary-world fantasy as a literary form** grounded in philological method: the fictional languages (Quenya, Sindarin) were constructed first, and the legendarium was written as the history required by those languages. The 1936 *Beowulf* lecture reframed the poem from a philological curiosity into a literary artifact, and remains foundational in Old English studies.

**Why canon.** The philology-first construction method is the novel formal move: a literary secondary world built as the consequent of constructed Indo-European-style language families. Every subsequent secondary-world fantasy literature is in this lineage.

**Downstream.** Entire genre of post-Tolkien fantasy literature; constructed-language (conlang) community; Old English studies.

**Tags.** `secondary-world` `philology-as-method` `conlang` `legendarium`
