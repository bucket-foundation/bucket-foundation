# 07 — Mind

> **What counts as canon-tier in mind.** A foundational claim about cognition, language, perception, or consciousness; a method that opens the empirical study of one of those; a framework (computational, ecological, predictive) that becomes the substrate of subsequent work. Refining a single experiment is not canon-tier. The figures below each forced or enabled a structural reframing of how mind is studied.

---

### Aristotle

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | aristotle |
| **lifespan** | 384–322 BCE |
| **era** | Classical Greece |
| **region / tradition** | Athens (Lyceum), Macedon — Greek philosophy & natural history |
| **branches** | 07-mind |
| **cross-branches** | 02-physics, 05-biophysics, 08-tradition, 10-earth |
| **primary works** | *De Anima* (c.350 BCE, Koine Greek); *Posterior Analytics*; *Nicomachean Ethics*; *Historia Animalium*; *Physics* |

**Foundation contribution.** Founded **systematic categorization** as a method (the categories, the predicables, the syllogism) — the substrate of every later taxonomy. *De Anima* gives the first systematic theory of the soul as the *form* of a living body, distinguishing nutritive, sensitive, and rational capacities and treating the mind as the activity of an organism rather than a separate substance. The *Posterior Analytics* gives the first theory of *demonstrative knowledge*: scientific knowledge is knowledge of *causes*, organized as deductions from first principles.

**Why canon.** Three foundations in three different fields (logic, biology, philosophy of mind), all of which set the agenda for subsequent inquiry for two millennia.

**Downstream.** All of medieval scholasticism; Aquinas; modern cognitive science (which is partly Aristotle minus the metaphysics); the entire taxonomy of the sciences.

**Tags.** `categories` `de-anima` `syllogism` `formal-cause`

### René Descartes

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | descartes |
| **lifespan** | 1596–1650 |
| **era** | Early modern |
| **region / tradition** | Paris, Netherlands, Stockholm — French rationalism |
| **branches** | 07-mind |
| **cross-branches** | 01-mathematics, 02-physics |
| **primary works** | *Discours de la méthode* (1637, French); *Meditationes de Prima Philosophia* (1641, Latin); *La Géométrie* (1637, French) |

**Foundation contribution.** Two foundations in two different branches: (1) **methodic doubt** and the *cogito* (*I think, therefore I am*) — the first-person standpoint as the secure starting point of philosophical inquiry, with the mind–body distinction it generates becoming the framing question for the next 400 years of philosophy of mind; (2) **analytic geometry** — translating geometric problems into algebraic equations via coordinates, the substrate that made calculus possible.

**Why canon.** The *Meditations* is the founding document of modern epistemology and philosophy of mind. *La Géométrie* is the founding document of analytic geometry. Each alone would be sufficient.

**Downstream.** Spinoza, Leibniz, Locke, Hume, Kant, all of modern Western philosophy; Newton, Leibniz, every later analytic geometer.

**Tags.** `cogito` `dualism` `analytic-geometry` `methodic-doubt`

### Immanuel Kant

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | kant |
| **lifespan** | 1724–1804 |
| **era** | Late Enlightenment |
| **region / tradition** | Königsberg — German idealism |
| **branches** | 07-mind |
| **primary works** | *Kritik der reinen Vernunft* (1781/1787, German); *Kritik der praktischen Vernunft* (1788); *Kritik der Urteilskraft* (1790) |

**Foundation contribution.** Argued in the *Critique of Pure Reason* that the structure of experience is partly imposed by the **a priori categories of the understanding** — space, time, causation, substance — which are not features of the world-in-itself but features of how a finite cognitive subject must structure any possible experience. The mind is *constitutive* of experience, not merely a passive recipient of it.

**Why canon.** Reframes the entire problem of knowledge: the question is no longer "do our representations match reality?" but "what must any cognitive subject be like to have the kinds of experience it has?" This is the founding move of cognitive science as a *transcendental* (now: computational) inquiry into the necessary conditions of cognition.

**Downstream.** Hegel, Schopenhauer, Husserl, the phenomenological tradition; Helmholtz, Marr, predictive-processing theories of perception (which are recognizably Kantian).

**Tags.** `a-priori` `categories` `transcendental` `synthetic-a-priori`

### William James

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | james |
| **lifespan** | 1842–1910 |
| **era** | Late 19th / early 20th century |
| **region / tradition** | Harvard — American philosophy & psychology |
| **branches** | 07-mind |
| **primary works** | *The Principles of Psychology* (1890, English); *Pragmatism* (1907) |

**Foundation contribution.** Founded **scientific psychology in the English-speaking world**. The *Principles* is the first major textbook to treat psychology as an experimental science alongside physiology, while taking introspective and phenomenological data seriously. Introduced the concept of the **stream of consciousness**, the **emotion-as-bodily-feeling** theory (James–Lange), and the *self* as a multi-layered process rather than a single thing.

**Why canon.** The bridge between philosophy of mind and the empirical psychology that follows. Almost every later figure in the cognitive sciences engaged with James, often by disagreement.

**Downstream.** Behaviorism (which rejected him), phenomenology (which absorbed him), modern consciousness studies, contemporary affective neuroscience.

**Tags.** `stream-of-consciousness` `pragmatism` `james-lange` `principles-of-psychology`

### Ludwig Wittgenstein

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | wittgenstein |
| **lifespan** | 1889–1951 |
| **era** | 20th century |
| **region / tradition** | Vienna, Cambridge — Austrian/English analytic philosophy |
| **branches** | 07-mind |
| **primary works** | *Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus* (1921, German); *Philosophical Investigations* (1953, German, posthumous) |

**Foundation contribution.** Two contradictory foundations: (1) the *Tractatus* gives a picture-theory of language in which meaningful sentences are pictures of possible facts, and the limits of language are the limits of thought; (2) the *Philosophical Investigations* repudiates the picture theory and develops the **language-games** framework — meaning is *use*, and language is a family of context-bound practices, not a logical mirror of the world.

**Why canon.** Two consecutive foundational positions, each of which became the basis of a major school. The *Tractatus* shaped the Vienna Circle and logical positivism. The *Investigations* shaped ordinary-language philosophy, the later Quine, and large parts of philosophy of mind and language. Few figures hold canon status for *replacing their own previous foundation*.

**Downstream.** Logical positivism (early); ordinary-language philosophy, Kripke, Searle, Brandom, much of philosophy of mind (later).

**Tags.** `language-games` `picture-theory` `meaning-is-use` `tractatus`

### Noam Chomsky

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | chomsky |
| **lifespan** | 1928– |
| **era** | Mid–late 20th century |
| **region / tradition** | MIT — American linguistics |
| **branches** | 07-mind |
| **cross-branches** | 04-information |
| **primary works** | *Syntactic Structures* (1957, English); *Aspects of the Theory of Syntax* (1965); review of Skinner's *Verbal Behavior* (1959) |

**Foundation contribution.** Founded **generative grammar**: a natural language is best modeled as a recursive system that can generate an unbounded set of well-formed sentences from a finite set of rules and a finite vocabulary. This recasts linguistics as a branch of formal language theory and computer science. The 1959 review of Skinner is widely cited as one of the events that ended behaviorism's dominance of psychology.

**Why canon.** Reframes language as a *computational* object, not a behavioral one. Defines the formal hierarchy (Chomsky hierarchy) of grammars that underlies both linguistics and the theory of programming-language parsing.

**Downstream.** All of generative linguistics; computational linguistics; the cognitive revolution; theoretical computer science (parsing, automata theory).

**Tags.** `generative-grammar` `chomsky-hierarchy` `cognitive-revolution` `universal-grammar`

### David Marr

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | marr |
| **lifespan** | 1945–1980 |
| **era** | Late 20th century |
| **region / tradition** | MIT, Cambridge — English/American computational neuroscience |
| **branches** | 07-mind |
| **cross-branches** | 04-information, 05-biophysics |
| **primary works** | *Vision: A Computational Investigation into the Human Representation and Processing of Visual Information* (1982, English, posthumous) |

**Foundation contribution.** Articulated the **three-level analysis** of any information-processing system: (1) **computational** — what problem is being solved and why; (2) **algorithmic** — what representations and procedures are used; (3) **implementational** — how the algorithm is realized in physical hardware (neural or silicon). Applied this framework to early vision, deriving (with Tomaso Poggio) computational accounts of edge detection, stereopsis, and surface reconstruction.

**Why canon.** The three-level framework is the standard way every subsequent computational neuroscientist talks about understanding a brain. Without it, the discipline lacks a shared vocabulary for the relationship between the function of a neural circuit and its biophysics.

**Downstream.** Computational neuroscience as a discipline; predictive-processing theories; the link between AI vision and biological vision.

**Disputed.** Died of leukemia at 35. *Vision* was finished by his colleagues from drafts.

**Tags.** `three-levels` `computational-vision` `marr-poggio` `levels-of-analysis`
