# 06 — Cosmology

> **What counts as canon-tier in cosmology.** A new model of the universe at scale (geocentric → heliocentric → expanding → inflationary), an observation that forces such a model change (Tycho's data, Hubble's redshifts, the CMB), or a theoretical apparatus that predicts cosmological structure (Friedmann equations, Lambda-CDM). Refining a parameter to higher precision is not canon-tier; the figures below each forced or enabled a structural reframing.

---

### Aristarchus of Samos

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | aristarchus |
| **lifespan** | c.310–c.230 BCE |
| **era** | Hellenistic |
| **region / tradition** | Samos, Alexandria — Greek astronomy |
| **primary works** | *On the Sizes and Distances of the Sun and Moon* (c.270 BCE, Koine Greek); the heliocentric proposal survives only via Archimedes's *Sand-Reckoner* |

**Foundation contribution.** Proposed the **first heliocentric model** in the Western tradition: the Sun is at the center, the Earth orbits the Sun, the stars are fixed at vast distance (which explains the absence of stellar parallax — they are simply too far). Estimated the Sun–Earth distance and the relative sizes of the Sun, Moon, and Earth using clean geometric arguments and lunar eclipse timings.

**Why canon.** The correct large-scale architecture of the solar system, 1,800 years before Copernicus. The model was rejected on physical and theological grounds, not for lack of evidence — exactly the kind of foundation a canon should preserve regardless of its century of acceptance.

**Disputed.** None of his original cosmological writing survives. Everything we know comes through Archimedes and later doxographers.

**Tags.** `heliocentrism` `solar-system` `parallax` `lost-original`

### Claudius Ptolemy

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | ptolemy |
| **lifespan** | c.100–c.170 CE |
| **era** | Roman Egypt |
| **region / tradition** | Alexandria — Greek/Roman astronomy |
| **primary works** | *Mathematike Syntaxis* (later known as the *Almagest*, c.150 CE, Koine Greek) |

**Foundation contribution.** Constructed a **complete predictive geocentric model** of planetary motion using nested deferents, epicycles, and equants. The model was *quantitatively predictive* — it could compute planetary positions accurately enough to be the working astronomical reference for fourteen centuries.

**Why canon.** The wrong model, but the foundational template for *what a predictive astronomical theory has to deliver*. Copernicus was rebuilding *against* the Almagest; Kepler was rebuilding *with* the same data tradition Ptolemy systematized. Canon does not mean "correct"; it means "the foundation everything subsequent had to engage with."

**Downstream.** All medieval Islamic and European astronomy; the Toledan, Alfonsine, and Prutenic tables; Copernicus's framing of his own work as a response to the Almagest.

**Tags.** `geocentric` `epicycle` `equant` `almagest`

### Aryabhata

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | aryabhata |
| **lifespan** | 476–550 CE |
| **era** | Classical India (Gupta period) |
| **region / tradition** | Pataliputra (Kusumapura) — Indian mathematical astronomy |
| **branches** | 06-cosmology |
| **cross-branches** | 01-mathematics |
| **primary works** | *Āryabhaṭīya* (499 CE, Sanskrit) |

**Foundation contribution.** Proposed that the **apparent rotation of the heavens is caused by the rotation of the Earth on its own axis**, and gave a value for the length of the sidereal day accurate to within minutes. Computed accurate values for $\pi$ (3.1416), the duration of the year, and the duration of the lunar month. Provided the trigonometric sine table that the Arabic astronomical tradition inherited and propagated.

**Why canon.** The rotating-Earth proposal, made in 499 CE, anticipates a structural feature of the modern view by a millennium. The trigonometric tables seeded the entire Islamic and (via translation) European astronomical traditions.

**Downstream.** Brahmagupta, the Arabic astronomical translation tradition, the medieval European inheritance via al-Battani and al-Biruni.

**Tags.** `earth-rotation` `sine-table` `pi` `indian-astronomy`

### Nicolaus Copernicus

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | copernicus |
| **lifespan** | 1473–1543 |
| **era** | Renaissance |
| **region / tradition** | Frombork (Royal Prussia) — Polish/German astronomy |
| **primary works** | *De revolutionibus orbium coelestium* (1543, Latin) |

**Foundation contribution.** Re-proposed and *systematized* the heliocentric model: the Sun is at the center of the planetary system; the Earth is one planet among others; the apparent motion of the stars is due to the Earth's own motion. The work is mathematically dense, conservative in its retention of circular orbits, and was published in the year of Copernicus's death.

**Why canon.** The hinge of early-modern astronomy. The "Copernican revolution" is the canonical example of a foundational reframing in which the *empirical content* changed less than the *organizing principle* did.

**Downstream.** Tycho (collected the data Kepler would need), Kepler (replaced circles with ellipses), Galileo (telescopic confirmation), Newton (mechanical explanation).

**Tags.** `heliocentrism` `de-revolutionibus` `copernican-principle` `early-modern-astronomy`

### Johannes Kepler

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | kepler |
| **lifespan** | 1571–1630 |
| **era** | Late Renaissance |
| **region / tradition** | Graz, Prague, Linz — German astronomy |
| **primary works** | *Astronomia Nova* (1609, Latin); *Harmonices Mundi* (1619, Latin) |

**Foundation contribution.** From Tycho Brahe's painstakingly precise naked-eye observations of Mars, derived the **three laws of planetary motion**: (1) orbits are ellipses with the Sun at one focus; (2) the line from the Sun to the planet sweeps out equal areas in equal times; (3) the square of the orbital period is proportional to the cube of the semi-major axis. The first law decisively replaces the millennia-old assumption of circular planetary orbits.

**Why canon.** The first quantitatively correct dynamical description of the solar system. Newton's universal gravitation was derived in part by *recovering* Kepler's laws from the inverse-square law.

**Downstream.** Newton, Laplace, all of celestial mechanics.

**Tags.** `keplers-laws` `ellipse` `area-law` `mars-orbit`

### Edwin Hubble

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | hubble |
| **lifespan** | 1889–1953 |
| **era** | Early 20th century |
| **region / tradition** | Mount Wilson Observatory, California — American observational astronomy |
| **primary works** | *Cepheids in Spiral Nebulae* (1925, English); *A Relation between Distance and Radial Velocity among Extra-Galactic Nebulae* (1929, English) |

**Foundation contribution.** Two foundations: (1) showed that "spiral nebulae" (notably Andromeda) are **independent galaxies** outside the Milky Way, multiplying the observable universe by orders of magnitude; (2) discovered that more distant galaxies are receding faster — the **Hubble–Lemaître law** $v = H_0 d$ — providing the observational basis for cosmic expansion.

**Why canon.** The size of the universe and the fact that it is *expanding* are both foundational identifications. Without these, general relativity is a beautiful equation in search of a cosmos to apply to; with them, modern cosmology has its first observational anchor.

**Downstream.** Lemaître's primeval atom (Big Bang model), all of observational cosmology, the cosmic distance ladder.

**Tags.** `galaxies` `cosmic-expansion` `hubble-law` `redshift`

### Georges Lemaître

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | lemaitre |
| **lifespan** | 1894–1966 |
| **era** | Early to mid-20th century |
| **region / tradition** | Louvain — Belgian theoretical cosmology (and Catholic priest) |
| **branches** | 06-cosmology |
| **cross-branches** | 02-physics |
| **primary works** | *Un univers homogène de masse constante et de rayon croissant rendant compte de la vitesse radiale des nébuleuses extra-galactiques* (1927, French); *L'hypothèse de l'atome primitif* (1931, French) |

**Foundation contribution.** Independently of Friedmann, derived an **expanding-universe solution** of Einstein's field equations, predicted the linear distance–velocity relation later attributed to Hubble, and proposed the **primeval atom** hypothesis — that the universe began from an extremely dense initial state and has been expanding since. This is the seed of the Big Bang model.

**Why canon.** First explicit expanding-universe cosmology grounded in GR + a physical origin scenario. Einstein famously dismissed the idea ("your math is correct but your physics is abominable") and later recanted.

**Downstream.** Gamow (nucleosynthesis), Penzias & Wilson (CMB discovery), the entire Big Bang / Lambda-CDM framework.

**Tags.** `expanding-universe` `primeval-atom` `big-bang` `friedmann-lemaitre`

### Vera Rubin

| | |
|---|---|
| **id** | rubin |
| **lifespan** | 1928–2016 |
| **era** | Late 20th century |
| **region / tradition** | DTM, Carnegie / Lowell Observatory — American observational astronomy |
| **primary works** | *Rotational Properties of 21 Sc Galaxies with a Large Range of Luminosities and Radii* (1980, English, with Ford & Thonnard) |

**Foundation contribution.** Showed that the **rotation curves of spiral galaxies are flat at large radii** — outer stars orbit at the same speed as inner stars, instead of slowing down as Keplerian gravity from the visible mass would predict. The only way to fit this is to assume a much larger mass than is visible — the observational founding of the modern **dark matter** hypothesis.

**Why canon.** Forces the recognition that ~85% of the matter in the universe is *not the matter we know*. The full theoretical resolution is still open — but the *existence of the discrepancy* is canon.

**Downstream.** All of dark-matter cosmology; Lambda-CDM; modified-gravity alternatives (MOND, etc.) which still must reproduce Rubin's curves.

**Tags.** `dark-matter` `galaxy-rotation` `flat-rotation-curve` `missing-mass`
