# Brand Workshop Framework

> A reusable 12-step playbook for building a brand from scratch
> Derived from: Jackson (Purpose/Position/Personality), Neumeier (Onlyness), Miller (StoryBrand), Hanlon (Primal Code), Placek/Lexicon (Naming), Jung/Mark & Pearson (Archetypes)
> Tested on: DerbyFish / Kala (verified fishing platform)

---

## How to Use This Document

This is a founder-led brand workshop designed to be completed in one sitting with an AI collaborator. The process takes 3-6 hours. Every step builds on the previous one — do them in order. Skip nothing.

**Who runs it:** The founder (you). You bring the conviction, the market knowledge, and the taste. The AI brings structure, volume (especially for naming), and pattern-matching against frameworks.

**What you need before you start:**
- A clear understanding of what your product does
- Knowledge of your competitive landscape
- Access to an AI assistant (Claude, GPT, etc.)
- A quiet room and no Slack notifications

**What you'll have when you're done:**
- A locked brand foundation document (Purpose, Enemy, Audience, Positioning, Personality, Story, Primal Code, Archetype, Voice, SOCO/SOCA, Taglines, Name)
- A creative brief ready for any designer or agency
- A naming shortlist with trademark and domain status

---

## Phase 1: Foundation (Steps 1-4)

These four steps establish what you are, what you fight, who you serve, and why you're the only one.

---

### Step 1: Purpose

**The question:** "The world will be a better place if ___."

**Framework:** Arielle Jackson's Big ideaL (originated at Ogilvy & Mather). This is your 10-year horizon — the thing that doesn't change when your product pivots, your pricing shifts, or your team turns over.

**How to run it:**

1. Draw a Venn diagram (mentally or on paper):
   - **Circle 1 — Cultural tension:** What's broken in the world that's relevant to your company? What's the systemic failure that people accept as normal?
   - **Circle 2 — Your brand's best self:** What does your company deliver when everything is working perfectly?
   - **Intersection:** Your purpose lives here.

2. Complete the sentence: "For our company, the world will be a better place if ___."

3. Pressure-test it:
   - Does it sustain for 10 years? If your product disappeared tomorrow, would the purpose still matter?
   - Is it bigger than your product? Purpose is not a feature description.
   - Would your competitors say the same thing? If yes, go deeper.

**What "done" looks like:** One sentence. No jargon. A child could understand the intent even if they don't understand the domain.

**Common mistakes:**
- Writing a mission statement instead of a purpose ("We help anglers track catches" is a mission; "The world's biggest sport deserves real infrastructure" is a purpose)
- Making it too narrow (tied to a specific product) or too broad (world peace)
- Confusing purpose with positioning — purpose is the 10-year "why," positioning is the 18-month "what"

**DerbyFish example:** "The world will be a better place if the world's biggest recreational sport had the infrastructure and league to match." This works because 57.9 million Americans fish — more than golf, tennis, and soccer combined — but the sport has no unified verification, no league, no persistent rankings, no spectacle. The purpose is bigger than any single feature.

---

### Step 2: Enemy

**The question:** What are you fighting against? What's broken?

**Framework:** Enemy Marketing (drawn from Adam Morgan's "Eating the Big Fish" and Donald Miller's StoryBrand villain concept). The enemy is never a competitor — it's the absence, the system failure, the thing everyone tolerates because nobody's fixed it.

**How to run it:**

1. List everything that's wrong with the current state of your industry. Don't filter. Get angry. The founder's frustration is raw material.

2. Identify the structural enemy — not "Company X is bad" but "the system is broken because ___."

3. Frame it as a single statement: "There is no ___ that ___. That's why ___. That's the enemy."

4. Validate: Does this enemy create urgency? Does it make someone nod and say "yeah, that's exactly right"?

**What "done" looks like:** A clear, specific articulation of what's broken — not who's bad, but what doesn't exist. One paragraph, max.

**Common mistakes:**
- Naming a competitor as the enemy (this is petty and fragile — they can change)
- Being too abstract ("the status quo" is not an enemy, it's a cliche)
- Describing a problem only your product has (the enemy should resonate even with people who've never heard of you)

**DerbyFish example:** "There is no system that turns a verified catch into competition, content, and entertainment in one motion. That's why fishing has no league. That's the enemy." The enemy isn't TourneyX or FishBrain — it's the absence of the connective tissue. Tournament software doesn't connect to social. Verification standards are fragmented. Entertainment costs a fortune because nothing is unified.

---

### Step 3: Audience

**The question:** Who is this for — specifically? And who is it NOT for?

**Framework:** Jackson's audience definition combined with Miller's hero identification. You need three layers: core audience, power niche, and explicit exclusions.

**How to run it:**

1. **Core audience:** Describe the person, not the demographic. What do they do? What do they already believe? What's the gap between what they have and what they want? Write it as a story — a day in their life, the moment of frustration, what they reach for and don't find.

2. **Power niche:** The tiny, fanatical subset that will build on top of your platform, evangelize without being asked, and punch far above their weight in influence. This group is often at an unlikely intersection (e.g., "developers who fish").

3. **"Not for" list:** Explicitly name who you're excluding. This is the hardest part for founders because exclusion feels like leaving money on the table. It's not — it's how positioning works. If you're for everyone, you're for no one.

4. **The feeling we create:** Write one sentence that captures how the user feels after using your product. Not a feature list — the emotional state.

**What "done" looks like:** Three clearly defined audience segments (core, power niche, not-for) plus the feeling statement. The core audience description should be vivid enough that someone reading it either says "that's me" or "that's definitely not me."

**Common mistakes:**
- Defining audience by demographics alone ("males 25-45" tells you nothing useful)
- Refusing to exclude anyone ("we're for all anglers!" means you're for none)
- Describing the audience you wish you had instead of the audience you actually have
- Skipping the power niche — this is where your early traction lives

**DerbyFish example:** Core = "The Gamer-Angler" — someone who tracks every catch but has no rank, who has an ELO in chess or a rank in Fortnite but nothing in the sport they spend the most money on. Power niche = "The Developer-Angler" — writes code AND fishes, sees an SDK and thinks "I want to build on this." Not for = solitude anglers, tech-resistant anglers, anti-gamification purists, old-school tournament diehards. Feeling = "Every catch matters. Every catch is verified. Every angler has a competitive identity that's earned, portable, and permanent."

---

### Step 4: Onlyness

**The question:** "We are the ONLY ___ that ___."

**Framework:** Marty Neumeier's Onlyness Statement from "The Brand Gap" / "Zag." This is the simplest and most brutal positioning test. If your competitors can honestly fill in the same blank, you don't have positioning — you have a marketing claim.

**How to run it:**

1. Complete the sentence: "[Company] is the ONLY [category] that [differentiator]."

2. Read it out loud. Then ask:
   - Can any competitor say this truthfully? If yes, rewrite.
   - Does it capture what makes you structurally different, not just operationally better?
   - Is the differentiator a compound capability (multiple things combined) or a single feature? Compound is stronger.

3. Test it against your top 5 competitors, one by one. For each, ask: "Could [Competitor X] say this?" If they can, your onlyness is wrong.

**What "done" looks like:** One sentence. No qualifiers. Passes the competitor test.

**Common mistakes:**
- Using subjective qualifiers ("the ONLY platform that truly cares about..." — "truly" is a red flag)
- Claiming onlyness based on a feature that can be copied in 6 months
- Making it too long — if you need a paragraph to explain your onlyness, you don't have one

**DerbyFish example:** "DerbyFish is the ONLY fishing platform that turns a verified fish into competition, content, and entertainment in one motion." Tournament platforms don't have verification. Social apps don't have leagues. Verification tools don't have competition or entertainment. Nobody has all of it in one motion.

## Phase 2: Character (Steps 5-8)

These steps define how your brand behaves, speaks, and feels.

---

### Step 5: Personality

**The question:** If your brand were a person, what three adjectives would describe them?

**Framework:** Arielle Jackson's Personality definition. Three adjectives — no more, no less. Each must be distinct (no synonyms). Together they should create tension — if all three adjectives are in the same family, your brand is one-dimensional.

**How to run it:**

1. Brainstorm 10-15 adjectives that describe how your brand should feel. Don't filter yet.

2. Cluster them. You'll find natural groupings. Pick one adjective from each of the three strongest clusters.

3. For each adjective, write a one-sentence definition in YOUR context (not the dictionary definition). What does "fun" mean for a fishing platform vs. a children's toy vs. a fintech app?

4. Test the trio: Does each adjective pull in a slightly different direction? Good. That tension is what makes the brand feel real and human. If all three say the same thing ("trustworthy, reliable, dependable"), you have one trait pretending to be three.

5. Define the relationship between brand and user: Is the brand the star, or the stage? The performer, or the spotlight? This determines whether personality shows up in how the brand talks about itself or how it talks about the user.

**What "done" looks like:** Three adjectives with contextual definitions + a clear statement of the brand-user relationship.

**Common mistakes:**
- Picking safe, undifferentiated adjectives ("innovative, passionate, trusted" — every startup says this)
- Picking adjectives that don't create tension (all from the same emotional family)
- Forgetting to define what each adjective means in your specific context
- Making the brand the star when it should be the stage

**DerbyFish example:** Legit, Fun, Cool. "Legit" = trusted, verified, precise, authoritative, but in the user's language, not the boardroom. "Fun" = seasons, loot, leaderboards, bragging rights — it's a game. "Cool" = the tech is impressive, the design is tight, people want to show it to their friends. The brand is the stage, not the performer — DerbyFish doesn't brag; DerbyFish gives YOU the receipts to brag with.

---

### Step 6: Story

**The question:** Where did you come from, and what's the narrative arc your user lives through?

**Framework:** Two layers. First, the founder origin story (raw, real, specific). Second, Donald Miller's StoryBrand (SB7) narrative framework applied to the user's journey.

**How to run it:**

**Part A — Origin Story:**

1. Tell it out loud. Don't write it — speak it. Record yourself if needed. The best origin stories sound like someone at a bar telling you how they accidentally started a company.

2. Find the specific detail. Not "we saw a problem in the market" but "Chuck said his nephew was a builder and could digitize the whole thing." Specificity is what makes stories believable.

3. Identify the pivot moment — the instant it went from "a project" to "this is bigger than we thought."

4. Write it in 150-200 words. If you can't tell it that short, you don't know what the story is yet.

**Part B — StoryBrand (SB7):**

Map your user's journey through Miller's 7 elements:

| Element | Question |
|---|---|
| **Hero** | Who is the user? (Not you — you are never the hero.) |
| **Problem** | What's the external problem, internal frustration, and philosophical injustice? |
| **Guide** | How does your brand show up? (With empathy AND authority.) |
| **Plan** | What are the simple steps? (3 steps max.) |
| **Call to Action** | What do you want them to do? |
| **Success** | What does life look like if they act? |
| **Failure** | What happens if they don't? |

**What "done" looks like:** A tight origin story (150-200 words) + a completed SB7 narrative map with all 7 elements filled in.

**Common mistakes:**
- Making the founder the hero of the StoryBrand (the user is ALWAYS the hero; you are the guide)
- An origin story that sounds like a press release instead of a bar story
- Skipping the "failure" element — this is what creates urgency
- Making the plan too complicated (if it's more than 3 steps, simplify)

**DerbyFish example:** Origin = Chuck and Matt bought WalleyeFest, Gian and Anthony digitized it from a garage, shipped it in 6 weeks, then realized the problem was every tournament on every lake — there was no verification layer, no league, no infrastructure. StoryBrand Hero = the gamer-angler with no rank. Problem = external: catching a fish and doing something meaningful with it are two separate worlds; internal: "I know I'm good but I have nothing to show for it"; philosophical: a sport this big should not be this invisible. Guide = DerbyFish, born from Oneida Lake. Plan = Catch a fish. DerbyFish handles the rest. Success = verified, ranked, climbing, watched. Failure = another photo nobody believes, another tournament nobody saw.

### Step 7: Primal Code + Archetype

**The question:** What are the tribal elements that turn users into believers? And what mythic role does your brand play?

**Framework:** Patrick Hanlon's 7 pieces of Primal Code + Jung/Mark & Pearson's 12 Brand Archetypes.

**How to run it:**

**Part A — Primal Code:**

Go through Hanlon's 7 pieces and fill in each one. Some will be obvious. Others will reveal gaps you didn't know you had.

| Piece | What It Is | How to Find It |
|---|---|---|
| **Creation Story** | How did this begin? | Comes from Step 6. Compress your origin story to one line. |
| **Creed** | The rallying cry. What do believers say? | What's the thing your most passionate user would put on a bumper sticker? |
| **Icons** | Visual and sensory markers. | Logo, colors, sounds, recurring visual elements — what's instantly recognizable? |
| **Rituals** | Repeated behaviors that create habit. | What does a user do every time they open the app? What's the daily/weekly rhythm? |
| **Sacred Words** | Vocabulary that only insiders use. | What terms does your community use that outsiders don't understand? |
| **Pagans** | Who's NOT in the tribe? | Comes from Step 3's "Not For" list. Who do believers define themselves against? |
| **Leader** | Who leads the community? | Founder(s), community members, or the community itself? |

**Part B — Archetype:**

Choose 1 primary and 1 secondary archetype from the 12:

| Archetype | Core Drive | Brand Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Innocent | Safety, simplicity | Coca-Cola, Dove |
| Explorer | Freedom, discovery | Patagonia, Jeep |
| Sage | Understanding, truth | Google, BBC |
| Hero | Mastery, courage | Nike, Under Armour |
| Outlaw | Liberation, disruption | Harley-Davidson, Virgin |
| Magician | Transformation | Apple, Disney |
| Regular Guy | Belonging | IKEA, Target |
| Lover | Intimacy, passion | Chanel, Hallmark |
| Jester | Enjoyment, humor | Old Spice, M&Ms |
| Caregiver | Service, protection | Johnson & Johnson, TOMS |
| Creator | Innovation, vision | LEGO, Adobe |
| Ruler | Control, order | Mercedes, Rolex |

The primary archetype drives your main narrative. The secondary adds texture and prevents the brand from being one-note.

**What "done" looks like:** All 7 Primal Code pieces filled in + primary and secondary archetypes selected with brief rationale for each.

**Common mistakes:**
- Leaving "Sacred Words" empty — if you don't have insider vocabulary, you don't have a community yet (so create it)
- Picking an archetype because it sounds cool rather than because it fits the actual user experience
- Choosing two archetypes that are too similar (Hero + Ruler are close; Hero + Outlaw creates real tension)
- Forcing a creed that's corporate instead of tribal ("Empowering anglers through technology" vs. "Verified or bullshit")

**DerbyFish example:** Primal Code creed = "Verified or bullshit." Sacred words = BAIT, VFS, FishCard, FishDex, Captain, Season, One Motion. Rituals = start session (core ritual), verify moment, captain review, check leaderboard, share FishCard. Primary archetype = Hero (the platform is the arena; every angler is proving themselves). Secondary = Outlaw (called bullshit on the honor system, open-sourcing the standard — more Linux than Harley).

### Step 8: Voice Calibration

**The question:** How does each personality trait actually sound in copy?

**Framework:** Jackson's voice calibration table. For each of your three personality adjectives, write four versions of the same message.

**How to run it:**

1. Pick a representative user moment — something your product does that you'll communicate about frequently. (e.g., "User just submitted a catch" or "User's trial is expiring")

2. For each personality trait, write the same message four ways:

| Version | What It Is |
|---|---|
| **Boring** | The flat, functional version. No personality at all. |
| **Without [trait]** | What the message sounds like if this specific trait is completely absent but others remain. |
| **Too Much** | The trait cranked to 11. Obnoxious, cringe, over the top. |
| **Just Right** | The Goldilocks zone. This is your actual brand voice for this trait. |

3. After writing all three tables, write a one-sentence summary of what each trait "sounds like" in practice.

4. The final voice is a BLEND of all three traits. Context determines which trait leads — a verification confirmation leans on Legit; a season drop announcement leans on Fun; a product launch leans on Cool.

**What "done" looks like:** Three complete calibration tables (one per trait) + a summary sentence per trait + a note on how blending works.

**Common mistakes:**
- Making "Just Right" sound like a compromise instead of its own confident voice
- Writing "Too Much" as a strawman that's easy to dismiss — it should be genuinely tempting, just slightly past the line
- Not testing the voice on real copy (push notifications, error messages, onboarding screens — not just marketing taglines)

**DerbyFish example:** See the full tables in BRAND-FOUNDATION.md Section 9. Key insight: "Legit sounds like a system that knows exactly what happened. Fun sounds like a hype man, not a show-off. Cool sounds like someone who doesn't use exclamation marks."

## Phase 3: Expression (Steps 9-12)

These steps produce the external-facing outputs: positioning statement, messaging guardrails, taglines, and the name.

---

### Step 9: Positioning Statement

**The question:** In one paragraph, how do you position yourself relative to the market?

**Framework:** Arielle Jackson's formal positioning template. This is an 18-month horizon statement — unlike Purpose (10 years), positioning evolves as your product and market evolve.

**How to run it:**

Fill in this template:

> For [target customer] who [customer need], [product name] is a [product category] that [key benefit]. Unlike [competitor alternative], we [primary differentiation].

Guidelines:
- **Target customer:** Use your core audience from Step 3, compressed to one phrase
- **Customer need:** The internal problem from Step 6, not the external one
- **Product category:** Choose carefully — this defines the competitive set you're evaluated against
- **Key benefit:** Your onlyness from Step 4, reworded as a benefit
- **Competitor alternative:** Not a single company — the category of alternatives
- **Primary differentiation:** What you do that they structurally cannot

**What "done" looks like:** One paragraph, no bullet points, no jargon. Should be readable by someone who knows nothing about your industry.

**Common mistakes:**
- Using buzzwords in the category ("AI-powered SaaS platform" — be specific)
- Listing multiple competitors instead of competitor categories
- Making the differentiation about a feature instead of a structural advantage
- Writing it as marketing copy instead of internal guidance (this is for your team and contractors, not your users)

**DerbyFish example:** "For gamer-anglers who intuitively understand seasons, ladders, and ranked competition but have no rank, no league, and no proof in fishing, DerbyFish is a verified fishing platform that turns a caught fish into competition, content, and entertainment in one motion. Unlike tournament software that stops at scoring, social apps that stop at posting, and catch loggers that go nowhere, DerbyFish owns the entire flow — verified, ranked, shareable, and live — in a single system."

---

### Step 10: SOCO / SOCA

**The question:** What's the ONE thing you want people to understand? And the ONE thing you never want them to think?

**Framework:** Arielle Jackson's Single Overriding Communications Objective / Avoidance. Everything you say, write, design, and build should orbit the SOCO. Everything should actively avoid the SOCA.

**How to run it:**

1. **SOCO:** Complete this sentence: "If someone spends 5 seconds with our brand and walks away understanding one thing, it's ___."
   - This is not your tagline. It's not your positioning. It's the single idea that sits underneath everything.
   - Test: Can you trace every feature, every piece of copy, every design choice back to this one idea? If something doesn't connect, it's off-brand.

2. **SOCA:** Complete this sentence: "The one misunderstanding that would kill our positioning is if people thought we were ___."
   - This is the category mistake, the false equivalence, the "oh, it's just another ___" that strips away everything unique about you.
   - Test: Look at your competitor landscape. What category would you be lumped into if your positioning failed? That's your SOCA.

**What "done" looks like:** Two sentences — one SOCO, one SOCA. Both should be immediately obvious to anyone who's done Steps 1-9.

**Common mistakes:**
- Making the SOCO a feature ("DerbyFish has AI verification") instead of a concept ("DerbyFish is verified fishing")
- Making the SOCA too broad ("we don't want people to think we're bad") instead of specific ("we don't want people to think we're just another tournament app")
- Having multiple SOCOs (the "O" in SOCO stands for "overriding" — there's only one)

**DerbyFish example:** SOCO = "DerbyFish is verified fishing." SOCA = "DerbyFish is just another tournament app." The creed ("Verified or bullshit") is the attitude. The SOCO is the understanding.

---

### Step 11: Tagline Family

**The question:** What are the verbal expressions of your brand across different contexts?

**Framework:** Hanlon's creed concept extended to a family of context-specific variants. A single tagline can't do everything — you need variants that share DNA but fit different registers.

**How to run it:**

1. Start with the internal creed — the raw, unfiltered version that your team says behind closed doors. This is your true north. It can be profane, aggressive, or weird. It just has to be true.

2. Create variants for each context:

| Context | Register | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Internal creed** | Raw, unfiltered | For the team. Can be profane. |
| **Public tagline** | Clean but punchy | App store, website header, pitch deck. |
| **Ad hook / commercial** | Provocative, question-format works well | Stops the scroll. Makes someone curious. |
| **App / youth / app store** | Approachable, PG | No profanity, no jargon. |
| **Merch / stickers** | Short, badge-worthy | Would someone put this on a laptop? |
| **Social** | Hashtaggable | Must work as a hashtag and as standalone text. |
| **B2B / sponsors** | Professional but still branded | For sponsor decks, partnership materials. |

3. Test each variant: Does it clearly descend from the internal creed? If you removed the logo, would someone still know it's your brand?

**What "done" looks like:** A completed table with 6-7 variants, all sharing the same DNA.

**Common mistakes:**
- Starting with the public tagline and working backward (always start from the raw creed)
- Making the B2B variant so sanitized it could belong to any company
- Making the merch variant too long (4 words max for merch)
- Creating variants that don't share a recognizable thread

**DerbyFish example:** Internal = "Verified or bullshit." Public = "Verified or nah." Ad hook = "But is it verified?" App/youth = "Verified or it didn't happen." Merch = "Verified or BS." Social = "#VerifiedOrNah." B2B = "Verified or just a story." All orbit the same core tension: verification vs. empty claims.

### Step 12: Naming

**The question:** What do you call this thing?

**Framework:** David Placek / Lexicon Branding method. This is the most labor-intensive step and the one where AI adds the most value. The key principle: generate volume first, judge later.

**How to run it:**

**Phase A — Generate (AI-heavy):**

1. Extract every noun, verb, and adjective from your positioning statement, purpose, and creed.

2. Build synonym lists for each word (thesaurus + conceptual associations).

3. Generate candidates across three buckets:
   - **Descriptive:** Explicitly describes the function (e.g., Whole Foods, PayPal)
   - **Suggestive:** Uses metaphor to evoke meaning (e.g., Amazon, Mint, Strava)
   - **Fanciful:** No direct connection to the offering (e.g., Apple, Adobe)

4. For each bucket, try these generation methods:
   - Real words (English and foreign languages)
   - Compound words (BlackBerry, PowerBook)
   - Blends / portmanteaus (Pinterest, Groupon)
   - Affixed words (Spotify, Shopify)
   - Truncations (Cisco, Tumblr)
   - Mythology / historical references (Palantir, Nike)
   - Deep-root foreign words (Strava = Swedish "strive"; Kala = Finnish "fish")

5. Target: 1,000+ candidates minimum. The AI can do the heavy lifting here — give it your brand foundation and ask for 200 candidates per bucket.

**Phase B — Filter (Founder-heavy):**

1. First pass: gut reaction. Does the name make you feel something? Keep anything that creates a reaction — even discomfort.

2. Apply Placek's principles:
   - **Discomfort signals success.** If the team is uncomfortable, it might be right.
   - **Polarization indicates strength.** If people are split, there's energy in the name.
   - **The competitor test:** "Our competitor just launched with this name — what's your reaction?" If the answer is "fuck, they took our name," it's a strong candidate.

3. Filter for practical requirements:
   - 2-3 syllables (optimal for recall)
   - Easy to spell after hearing it once (the "phone test")
   - Works in multiple languages (no unintended meanings)
   - Doesn't sound like an existing brand in adjacent categories

**Phase C — Validate:**

1. **Trademark search:** USPTO TESS (tmsearch.uspto.gov) for US, WIPO Global Brand Database for international. Check your relevant Nice classes (likely 9, 41, 42 for software/sports/SaaS).

2. **Domain availability:** Check primary domain (.com, relevant TLD like .fish, .io). Remember Placek's advice: "Get the right name first, solve for domain later." Alternative TLDs, prefixes (get, try, use), and negotiated purchases are all valid.

3. **Social handle availability:** Check Twitter/X, Instagram, TikTok, YouTube.

4. **Linguistic check:** Run the finalist through native speakers of major languages. Ask specifically about negative associations.

**What "done" looks like:** A shortlist of 3-5 finalists with trademark status, domain availability, and linguistic notes for each. One founder-selected winner with a brief rationale.

**Common mistakes:**
- Judging names too early in the generation phase (quantity comes first, quality comes second)
- Picking the "safe" name over the one that creates energy
- Letting domain availability kill a great name (domains can be acquired; greatness can't)
- Naming by committee instead of founder conviction
- Skipping the trademark search (a $50K rebrand later is much more expensive than a $400 TESS search now)
- Using a name generator tool and stopping at 50 candidates (you need 1,000+)

**DerbyFish example:** 15,777 programmatically generated candidates -> 327 deep-root foreign/mythology names -> hand-curated shortlist -> founder selection of "Kala" (Finnish for "fish"). Two syllables, open vowels, easy in every language. Passes the competitor test. Trademark clear in USPTO Classes 9, 41, 42. Primary domain: kala.fish (available).

## Post-Workshop: What Happens Next

Once the brand foundation is locked, the next steps are:

1. **Creative Brief** — Package Steps 1-12 into Jackson's creative brief format (one-to-two pages) for any designer or agency.

2. **Visual Identity** — Logo, color palette, typography, icon system. Give the designer the personality adjectives, the archetype, and the Primal Code icons as inputs.

3. **Domain Acquisition** — Secure primary and backup domains.

4. **Trademark Filing** — File USPTO application, then Madrid Protocol for international protection.

5. **Brand Guidelines Document** — Expand the voice calibration tables into a full brand guidelines document with do/don't examples for every communication channel.

6. **App Store / Public Rebrand** — If transitioning from a working name (like DerbyFish -> Kala), plan the transition: legal entity, app store listing, social handles, redirect strategy.

## One Last Thing

The hardest part of brand building is not generating ideas — it's making decisions. Every step in this framework ends with a deliverable that requires a decision. The AI can generate 15,000 name candidates, but the founder picks one. The AI can write 10 versions of a creed, but the founder knows which one is true.

Brand is founder conviction expressed as a system. This framework gives you the system. You bring the conviction.
